Ada Lovelace

Augusta Ada King, Countess of Lovelace, was an English mathematician and writer.
She is chiefly known for her work on Charles Babbage's proposed mechanical general-purpose computer, the Analytical Engine.
Lovelace is often described as the first computer programmer.

Lovelace translated an article by the military engineer Luigi Menabrea about the Analytical Engine.
She then added seven long notes to the translation, and those notes were much longer than the translated article itself.
Her notes described a method for using the machine to calculate Bernoulli numbers, often called the first published computer program.

She went beyond seeing the machine as only a calculator.
Lovelace argued that computing devices could manipulate symbols and information other than numbers.
She explicitly discussed possibilities such as music and other encoded information.
She described her own approach as "poetical science".

Lovelace's work centered on Babbage's Analytical Engine rather than an electronic computer.
Although the engine was never built in her lifetime, later historians credited her with recognizing ideas that anticipated modern general-purpose computing.

Alan Turing

Alan Turing was an English mathematician, computer scientist, logician, cryptanalyst, philosopher, and theoretical biologist.
He is widely considered a foundational figure in theoretical computer science.
Turing formalized the concepts of algorithm and computation with the Turing machine.

During World War II, Turing worked for the Government Code and Cypher School at Bletchley Park.
He led Hut 8, the section responsible for German naval cryptanalysis.
He played a major role in work against Enigma and in techniques that accelerated Allied codebreaking.

After the war, Turing worked at the National Physical Laboratory and designed the Automatic Computing Engine, one of the first stored-program computer designs.
Later he worked at the University of Manchester on early computers.
He also became associated with the Turing test and with major work in mathematical biology.
His research on morphogenesis examined how biological patterns could arise from chemical processes.

In 1952, Turing was prosecuted for homosexual acts.
He accepted hormone treatment rather than prison.
He died in 1954 from cyanide poisoning.
Decades later, the British government issued an apology and Queen Elizabeth II granted him a posthumous pardon.

Grace Hopper

Grace Hopper was an American computer scientist, mathematician, and United States Navy rear admiral.
She earned degrees from Vassar and Yale and later became a professor of mathematics before joining the Navy Reserve during World War II.

Hopper worked on the Harvard Mark I as part of the Bureau of Ships Computation Project.
She was among the early programmers for that system and co-authored papers on the machine.
She is also associated with the famous story of a moth found in a relay and the popularization of the term "debugging".

In 1949 Hopper joined the Eckert-Mauchly Computer Corporation and later worked on UNIVAC.
She developed one of the earliest compiler-related systems, including the A-0 system.
She strongly argued that programming should move toward machine-independent, English-like languages rather than raw symbolic or machine code.

Her work influenced FLOW-MATIC and COBOL.
Hopper helped promote the idea that data-processing languages should be readable by ordinary people, not just specialists in low-level machine instructions.
She remained a major public advocate for computing standards, programming-language portability, and practical software development.
