Project Mercury

Project Mercury was the first human spaceflight program of the United States.
It ran from 1958 through 1963.
Its goal was to put a man into Earth orbit and return him safely, ideally before the Soviet Union fully consolidated its lead in the Space Race.

Mercury used a very small capsule designed for a single astronaut.
It conducted uncrewed developmental flights and six successful crewed flights.
The program included both suborbital and orbital missions.
John Glenn became the first American to orbit Earth during Mercury.

Mercury established the basic American capability for crewed spaceflight.
Its success laid the groundwork for Project Gemini, which followed Mercury, and for the later Apollo program.

Project Gemini

Project Gemini was the second United States human spaceflight program.
It was conducted after Mercury and while Apollo was still in development.
Gemini ran from 1961 to 1966, with crewed missions in 1965 and 1966.

Gemini spacecraft carried a two-astronaut crew.
The program's main purpose was to develop the techniques needed for Apollo's lunar missions.
Those techniques included long-duration spaceflight, extravehicular activity, rendezvous, and docking.
Gemini also demonstrated orbital maneuvers and mission endurance long enough to support a lunar round trip.

Because Gemini focused on proving these operational techniques, it is often described as the bridge between Mercury and Apollo.
It allowed Apollo to pursue lunar landing goals without having to invent all of those methods from scratch during Apollo missions.

Apollo program

The Apollo program was NASA's crewed lunar exploration program.
It was conceived in 1960, reshaped after President John F. Kennedy's 1961 Moon goal, and executed from 1961 to 1972.
Apollo's purpose became landing humans on the Moon and returning them safely to Earth.

Apollo used lessons learned from Mercury and Gemini but had a broader and more ambitious mission profile.
The program first flew astronauts in 1968, reached lunar orbit with Apollo 8, and achieved the first Moon landing with Apollo 11 in 1969.
Neil Armstrong and Buzz Aldrin walked on the lunar surface while Michael Collins remained in lunar orbit.

Apollo ultimately landed astronauts on the Moon in six missions.
The program also suffered the Apollo 1 fire and the in-flight failure that forced Apollo 13 to abort its lunar landing attempt.
Even so, Apollo became the program most closely associated with crewed lunar landing rather than Earth-orbit technique development.
